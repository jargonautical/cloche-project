Clearing the fog

subheader

Posted by Start Bootstrap on August 24, 2014

There’s this about dealing with large amounts of data, open or otherwise; it’s a very complex art, requires detailed training and a solid understanding of some quite frankly weird mathematical tricks. It’s not something you can just jump into and master all in one go. With that established, it makes sense that the people we entrust with our data analysis need to be incredibly clever, highly trained and focused individuals who will bring scientific rigour and if we’re very lucky a touch of elegance to the way the data is manipulated and structured and stored. So far so good. There’s a more than adequate supply of data, and no apparent shortage of bright conscientious people. However, there’s an issue of accountability – if the only people who can understand the data are a subset of very clever people, who will ensure that the data is not used to mislead the rest of us? Additionally, we don’t yet have data views that tell us what we need to know. Where is the performance version of Citymapper or BBC Weather? Where is the data equivalent of Google search? These things don’t exist yet – or if they do they aren’t visible yet – and we say this is the biggest part of the problem. These tools should have been at the front end of the whole process, bringing us to the realisation of what it is people need the data to do for them and how they would like to consume it. Something can be complicated without being incomprehensible. Here’s probably the most instantly recognisable example:

“The first diagrammatic map of London’s rapid transit network was designed by Harry Beck in 1931. Beck was a London Underground employee who realised that because the railway ran mostly underground, the physical locations of the stations were irrelevant to the traveller wanting to know how to get to one station from another — only the topology of the railway mattered.” This is important – Beck made this connection and produced the first version of the map in his own time; existing official Underground maps contained all the overground geographical information too – because showing everything we know if the way to go, right? Wrong, as London Underground discovered when they grudgingly produced a limited run of leaflets with Beck’s design. This also enable a critical second thing to happen – millions of people use the Tube every day and can verify – first hand – that the representation above is accurate for its intended purpose. So we think that it’s possible to take the most complex mix of data sets and find a way to display it so that it’s useful to people for some real purpose. But there must be these three things – fitness for purpose, brevity and verifiability – baked into the design.
